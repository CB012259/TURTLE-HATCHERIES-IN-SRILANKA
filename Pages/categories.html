GREEN TURTLE


The Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas) is an enthralling species found in Sri Lanka's coastal waters. It is a unique turtle with a dark green carapace and a distinguishing look. Green turtles nest on the beaches of Sri Lanka, including Induruwa, Bentota, Kosgoda, and Hikkaduwa. They eat largely seagrasses and algae and are herbivorous. These turtles are well-known for their extensive travels and may be found in both shallow and deep seas. Despite challenges like as habitat degradation and inadvertent capture, conservation initiatives strive to conserve their nesting grounds and promote awareness about their role in maintaining the balance of the marine ecosystem.

HAWKSBILL TURTLE


The Hawksbill Turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata) is a severely endangered species that lives in Sri Lanka's coastal waters. It is a compelling turtle due to its distinct look, which includes a pointed beak and a beautifully patterned shell. Hawksbill Turtles are well-known for breeding on Sri Lankan beaches such as Kosgoda, Pigeon Island, Induruwa, and Hikkaduwa. They typically inhabit shallow coral reef environments, where they feed on sponges and play an important role in maintaining a healthy ecology. Despite several concerns, including as habitat degradation and poaching, conservation efforts in Sri Lanka focus on conserving their nesting places and raising awareness about their conservation requirements.

LOGGERHEAD TURTLE


The Loggerhead Turtle (Caretta caretta) is an enthralling species that is seldom seen in Sri Lanka's coastal waters. It is a stunning turtle with a reddish-brown carapace and a commanding appearance. Loggerhead Turtles lay their eggs in Sri Lankan beaches, particularly on Pigeon Island, Trincomalee, and Uppuveli. They are famous for their enormous size and long lifetime, with adults reaching lengths of up to 1 meter. Loggerhead Turtles mostly inhabit deep oceans and feed on crustaceans, mollusks, and fish. Despite problems like as habitat degradation and unintended capture, conservation efforts in Sri Lanka are focused on protecting nesting sites and increasing conservation awareness.

OLIVE RIDELY


The Olive Ridley Turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea) is a small and widespread species of sea turtle found in Sri Lankan coastal waters. It has a heart-shaped carapace with a grayish to olive-green coloration. It has a distinctive head with a slight hooked beak. During the nesting season, the turtles display a synchronized mass event known as an arribada, in which multiple females, sometimes hundreds or even thousands, arrive at the shore at the same time. Nesting sites in Sri Lanka, including Rekawa and Kosgoda, as well as Pottuvil and Panama, are essential for the survival of this species. The Olive Ridley Turtle is omnivorous and feeds on a variety of invertebrates, including jellyfish, shrimps, crabs, and marine urchins. It is an important part of the marine ecosystem and is a sight to behold during the nesting season.

LEATHERBACK TURTLE


The Leatherback Turtle (Dermochelys coriacea) can be seen in the coastal waters of Sri Lanka. Its unusual leather carapace, rather of a hard shell, makes it the biggest turtle in the world. This species is widely migratory and may be found on a number of beaches that are suitable for breeding, including Pigeon Island and Trincomalee. It has a smooth body with ridges running the length of its carapace. These turtles are vital to the wellbeing of the marine ecology and largely eat jellyfish. Conservation efforts are being made to safeguard their nesting locations and spread awareness of their endangered status.
